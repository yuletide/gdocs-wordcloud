Cities United: Building Communities to Reduce Deaths Among Black Men and Boys

Homicide = leading cause of death for AA males aged 15 to 24.

Children exposed to this violence suffer, along with the community at large, due to the decrease in the number of AA men remaining in the community

49% of all homicide victims in 2005 were black

Estimated that 10mm children/year may witness or become victims of violence in their homes

“When communities produce better boys, they produce better men.”

The Open Society Foundation’s Campaign for Black Male Achievement presents

a particular opportunity to bring focus to these efforts, to help spread this focus

nationwide, and to generate a national call to action. A collaboration between the

Open Society Foundation, the Knight Foundation, Casey Family Programs, the

National League of Cities, the U.S. Conference of Mayors, and other

organizations such as national faith, community, civic, other governmental and

municipal agencies, and fraternal organizations, and youth voices) could help to

begin and sustain the necessary conversations that would lead to specific cities

developing action strategies in their respective locales.

Objectives:

  1. Raise awareness of the issue
  2. Keep crisis at center of municipal conversation
  3. Create national attention
  4. Desired outcome is a reduction in the number of violence related deaths of AA males in the identified geographic areas

Approach:

  1. Strategy group - mayors, ministers, foundations, youth from tipping point cities
  2. Agenda needs to include key considerations like
  1. a) expanding the focus to achievement by men of color (including those on Native American reservations)
  2. b) understanding the intersections of youth education and families
  3. c) understanding different forms of partnerships
  4. d) learning about best practices and lifting up what works
  5. e) measuring what works
  6. f) providing leadership development and opportunities for mayoral mentoring
  7. g) understanding how to sustain these efforts.

  1. Hold a series of convening’s designed to help mayors transform the conditions of communities and reverse the trend where African American male presence is being depleted.
  2. Purpose is to assist cities in developing concrete action plans to align with existing efforts, to initiate new efforts, and to encourage a national call to action.
  3. In addition to raising awareness and receiving technical assistance utilizing the toolkit that the National League of Cities is developing, a set of recommendations would be developed designed to:
  1. a) Encourage the inclusion of a focus on African American males in existing initiatives where one does not exist.
  2. b) Establish new foci in additional cities/communities.
  3. c) Frame the role and deliverables for a Presidential Commission or Workgroup.

See the report for an impressive list of current efforts underway. (CitiesUnited.pdf)

Cities United: Building Communities to Reduce Deaths Among Black Men and Boys (Mayor’s Office)

From meeting 10/25/2011 in Philly at the Natl Constitution Center.

Meeting goals were to:

  1. Develop a shared sense of urgency to reduce violent related deaths among black males
  2. Understand promising practices and opportunities for municipal leadership to reduce violent related deaths
  3. Identify strategies for integrating authentic youth voice and leadership in any initiative focused on reducing violent deaths among black males
  4. Determine next steps for developing a national agenda for reducing violent related deaths among black males

How do we begin the natl conversation:

  1. Name the Challenge: Identifying obstacles and opportunities to serving black males in violence prevention efforts
  2. Unpack the Reality: Increasing understanding around the reasons for violent deaths, and how that disproportionately happens for black males
  3. Create Urgency: Developing a call to action for the need to address this issue with immediacy
  4. Dispel Myths: Providing information to change the perception of black males who are victims of violent-related deaths
  5. Understand and Implement What Works: Increasing knowledge about effective approaches currently being used

Other questions:

  1. What are your best practices in addressing violence?
  2. What innovative solutions exist in your cities?
  3. Who will be your point of contact for continued collaboration?

Black Victims of Violent Crime (Bureau of Labor Statistics)

From 2001 to 2005:

  1. Black males were more vulnerable to violent victimization than black females.
  2. Younger blacks were generally more likely than older blacks to be victims of violence.
  3. Blacks who had never married were more likely than all other blacks to be victims of violence.
  4. Blacks in households with lower annual incomes were at a greater risk of violence than those in households with higher annual incomes.
  5. Blacks living in urban areas were more likely than those in suburban or rural areas to be victims of violence.

Youth Violence (CDC)

Report is from 2010

  1. In 2007, 5,764 young people ages 10 to 24 were murdered—an average of 16 each day (CDC 2010a).
  2. Homicide was the 2nd leading cause of death for young people ages 10 to 24 years old (CDC 2010a).
  3. Among 10 to 24 year-olds, 86% (4,973) of homicide victims were male and 14% (791) were female (CDC 2010a).
  4. Among homicide victims ages 10 to 24 years-old, 84% were killed with a firearm (CDC 2010a).

See the report for more stats, not all violence related. (CitiesUnited_CDCYouthViolence.pdf)

Children’s Exposure to Violence: A Comprehensive National Survey (Juvenile Justice Bulletin)

Report is from data gathered Jan-May 2008

  1. This survey is the first comprehensive attempt to measure children’s exposure to violence in the home, school, and community across all age groups from birth to age 17,
  2. The survey confirms that most of our society’s children are exposed to violence in their daily lives.
  3. More than 60 percent of the children surveyed were exposed to violence within the past year, either directly or indirectly.
  4. Nearly one-half of the children and adolescents surveyed were assaulted at least once in the past year, and more than 1 in 10 (10.2 percent) were injured in an assault;
  5. 1 in 4 were victims of robbery, vandalism, or theft;
  6. 1 in 10 suffered from child maltreatment (including physical and emotional abuse, neglect, or a family abduction)
  7. 1 in 16 were victimized sexually.
  8. More than 1 in 4 witnessed a violent act
  9. Nearly 1 in 10 saw one family member assault another.
  10. Multiple victimizations were common.
  11. Nearly seven in eight children who reported being exposed to violence during their lifetimes also reported being exposed to violence within the past year, which indicated that these children were at ongoing risk of violent victimization.
